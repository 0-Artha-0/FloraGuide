Aloe Vera Plant (Aloe barbadensis)

Aloe Vera is a popular succulent known for its thick, fleshy leaves that contain a soothing gel often used for skincare and medicinal purposes. Native to arid regions, Aloe Vera thrives in bright light and requires minimal watering, making it a hardy, low-maintenance houseplant. It’s valued not only for its resilience but also for its natural healing properties, especially for burns and skin irritation. With proper care, Aloe Vera can live for years and even produce offsets, allowing for easy propagation.

Watering

Aloe Vera is a succulent and prefers to be watered infrequently, as it stores water in its thick leaves. The plant should be allowed to dry out completely between waterings.

Frequency

Aloe Vera needs infrequent but thorough watering. About every 2–3 weeks is sufficient, depending on the climate and humidity. Allow the soil to dry out completely between waterings, as Aloe Vera is drought-tolerant and can store water in its leaves. In winter, reduce watering frequency, as the plant’s growth naturally slows during this time

Technique

To avoid rot, water Aloe Vera at the base of the plant rather than on its leaves. Pour water slowly until it drains out of the pot’s holes, then discard any excess water in the saucer. Avoid splashing water on the leaves, as moisture can lead to fungal growth or rot at the base of the leaves.

Soil

Aloe Vera requires a soil that drains quickly to prevent root rot. A cactus or succulent soil mix works best because it’s designed for fast drainage. For a DIY mix, combine regular potting soil with coarse sand and perlite in equal parts. This mix mimics the plant’s natural desert habitat by preventing moisture from accumulating around the roots. Planting Aloe Vera in a terracotta pot with drainage holes can also help absorb excess water and maintain ideal conditions for the roots.

Light

Aloe Vera thrives in bright, indirect sunlight but can also handle a few hours of direct sunlight each day if it’s gradually exposed. Place it in a south- or west-facing window where it can receive 4-6 hours of light. If the plant doesn’t get enough light, it may start to stretch or grow leggy as it reaches toward the light.

Temperature

Aloe Vera prefers temperatures between 55–80°F (13–27°C) and can adapt well to most indoor environments. However, it’s sensitive to frost and should be brought indoors if temperatures drop below 50°F (10°C). In warmer climates, it can also thrive outdoors but needs protection from sudden temperature fluctuations or prolonged cold.

Fertilization

Aloe Vera doesn’t require heavy feeding. A balanced, diluted fertilizer applied once in the spring or summer is generally enough. Avoid fertilizing in the fall and winter when the plant’s growth slows. Using too much fertilizer can lead to salt buildup in the soil, which can harm the roots and affect the plant’s health.

Pruning

Aloe Vera doesn’t need frequent pruning, but you can trim off any yellowed, dead, or damaged leaves at the base. Use a clean, sharp pair of scissors or pruning shears to make a clean cut close to the soil line. If you plan to harvest leaves for their gel, avoid cutting too many healthy leaves at once, as this may stress the plant.

Humidity

Aloe Vera prefers low to moderate humidity levels, as it’s adapted to dry climates. Humidity isn’t usually a concern, and misting should be avoided, as this can introduce excess moisture that can lead to fungal issues or rot.

Air Circulation

Good airflow is important for preventing fungal issues and rot. Place Aloe Vera in a well-ventilated area but away from strong drafts or fans, which can dry it out too much. In humid climates, airflow helps to keep excess moisture from building up around the plant.

Pest Management

Aloe Vera is generally pest-resistant, but it can occasionally attract pests like mealybugs, aphids, or scale. Check the plant regularly for any signs of pests, such as small white spots or sticky residue on the leaves. If pests are present, you can gently wipe the leaves with a cloth and apply insecticidal soap or neem oil as needed. Removing pests early helps prevent infestations from spreading.

Repotting

Aloe Vera should be repotted every 1–2 years or when it becomes root-bound. Repotting in the spring allows the plant to establish itself in new soil before its active growing season. Choose a pot that’s one size larger than the current one, with drainage holes to prevent water from accumulating. When repotting, use fresh cactus or succulent soil to support healthy growth and provide the plant with fresh nutrients.
