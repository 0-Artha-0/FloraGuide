Snake Plant (Sansevieria)

The Snake Plant (Sansevieria), also known as “Mother-in-Law’s Tongue,” is a hardy, low-maintenance houseplant recognized for its tall, upright, sword-like leaves, which can be green or variegated with yellow or silver edges. It thrives in a range of lighting conditions, from low to bright indirect light, making it highly adaptable indoors. They prefer average room temperatures and low humidity. Known for its air-purifying qualities, the Snake Plant filters toxins and adds oxygen to indoor spaces.

Watering

Snake plants require minimal watering—about once every 2–4 weeks—allowing the soil to dry out between waterings, as they are prone to root rot if overwatered.

Frequency

Water sparingly. Let the soil dry completely between waterings to avoid root rot. Typically, every 2-3 weeks in the warmer months and even less in winter.

Technique

Water at the base, not on the leaves, and make sure the excess water drains freely from the pot.

Soil

Well-draining, sandy, or cactus soil. A mix of potting soil and perlite or sand works well.

Light

Tolerates low light but thrives in bright, indirect light. Avoid direct sunlight as it may scorch the leaves. This plant is very adaptable and thrives in low light conditions, making it an excellent choice for indoors.

Temperature

Prefers temperatures between 60-85°F (16-29°C). Avoid placing it in drafty areas or areas that drop below 50°F (10°C).

Fertilization

Fertilize once a month during the growing season (spring and summer) with a balanced, diluted liquid fertilizer. No fertilization is needed during fall and winter.

Pruning

Remove dead or damaged leaves at the base to maintain a tidy appearance. Pruning isn’t necessary for growth, as it grows slowly.

Humidity

Tolerates low humidity and doesn’t need extra humidity.

Air Circulation

Good air circulation is beneficial, but the snake plant isn’t particularly sensitive to air flow.

Pest Management

Snake plants are relatively pest-resistant but can occasionally be affected by mealybugs or spider mites.

Repotting

Repot when it outgrows its pot or when the roots become crowded. This typically happens every 1-2 years.
